01 Logic & Foundations

Introduction to Mathematical Logic

Elliott Mendelson · 1964

The standard entry point into propositional and predicate calculus. Rigorous and self-contained — a good companion before tackling proof theory or model theory.

Naive Set Theory

Paul Halmos · 1960

Deceptively titled. Halmos writes with exceptional clarity, and this slim volume remains the best way to build genuine intuition about sets before ZFC formalism.

02 Algebra

Algebra

Michael Artin · 2nd ed.

All the algebra a bachelor's student should know, done with the right level of abstraction. The linear algebra chapters complement the group theory beautifully.

Matrix Analysis

Horn & Johnson · 1985

The definitive reference for anyone working with matrix theory beyond the introductory level. Indispensable for spatial econometrics and spectral methods.

The Theory of Matrices, Vol. 1 & 2

Felix R. Gantmacher · 1959

A Soviet classic. The treatment of canonical forms, functions of matrices, and stability theory goes far beyond any modern linear algebra text — essential for advanced applications.

03 Geometry & Topology

Lezioni di Geometria, Vol. 1

Rosa Esposito, Assunta Russo · 1996

My first geometry book. Only in Italian, but it shaped how I think about mathematical space. Some debts are owed to very specific volumes.

Topology

James Munkres · 2nd ed.

The standard graduate text, and deservedly so. Munkres writes with unusual care; the problem sets are excellent. Covers point-set topology fully before moving to algebraic topology.

Introduction to Metric and Topological Spaces

Wilson Sutherland · 1975

A gentler entry point for those coming from analysis. The transition from metric to topological spaces is handled with real care.

04 Mathematical Analysis

Real and Complex Analysis

Walter Rudin · 1966

"Big Rudin." The chapters on measure and integration are a masterpiece of compression. Non-negotiable if you want a serious foundation for probability theory.

Introductory Real Analysis

A.N. Kolmogorov & S.V. Fomin · 1975

A more accessible companion to Rudin. The exposition of the Lebesgue integral is especially clear, and the exercises build intuition well.

Probability and Measure

Patrick Billingsley · 1979

The bridge between measure theory and probability. Billingsley's treatment of weak convergence and characteristic functions is the foundation you need for asymptotic econometrics.

Introductory Functional Analysis with Applications

Erwin Kreyszig · 1978

The most user-friendly door into functional analysis. Kreyszig never sacrifices rigour for accessibility, but he always explains why things matter before proving them.

Functional Analysis

Walter Rudin · 1973

For those who have survived the first two Rudins. The treatment of distributions, Fourier analysis, and operator theory here has no equal at this level of concision.

05 Dynamical Systems

Ordinary Differential Equations

Vladimir Arnold · 1978

Arnold approaches ODEs as a geometer, not an analyst. The emphasis on phase flows and qualitative behaviour is exactly the perspective economists applying dynamic systems need.

Nonlinear Dynamics and Chaos

Steven H. Strogatz · 2nd ed.

The most readable graduate text on dynamical systems. The treatment of stability and bifurcation theory is thorough and well-motivated, with applications drawn from across science.

06 Numerical Methods

Numerical Linear Algebra

Trefethen & Bau · 1997

A remarkable book: rigorous yet remarkably readable. The lectures on SVD, QR, and iterative methods make you understand what computation actually does to matrices.

Numerical Methods for Scientists and Engineers

R. W. Hamming · 1962

Old but not outdated. Hamming's philosophy — "the purpose of computing is insight, not numbers" — belongs on every quantitative researcher's wall.

07 Optimization

Convex Optimization

Boyd & Vandenberghe · 2004

Freely available online. The gold standard for convex analysis and optimization algorithms. Every chapter in Part II is directly applicable to estimation problems in econometrics and ML.

Nonlinear Programming

Dimitri Bertsekas · 2nd ed.

The most complete treatment of constrained optimization I have found. The chapters on Lagrangian duality and the KKT conditions are essential for understanding modern estimators.

Linear Programming and Economic Analysis

Dorfman, Samuelson & Solow · 1958

The authors start with the diet problem, leading the reader through the most important linear algorithms applied to economic theory. A beautiful marriage of mathematics and economics.

Statistics — Theory

Statistical Inference

Casella & Berger · 2nd ed.

Timeless. The treatment of sufficiency, completeness, and the Cramér-Rao bound is impeccable. Invaluable as a desk reference.

Asymptotic Statistics

Aad van der Vaart · 1998

Dense, exact, and beautiful. The chapters on empirical processes and the delta method are the backbone of modern econometric asymptotics. Not for the faint-hearted.

Testing Statistical Hypotheses

Lehmann & Romano · 3rd ed.

The definitive treatment of hypothesis testing from a decision-theoretic perspective. The UMP and UMPU results here clarify what most applied texts leave vague.

Statistics — Applied & Machine Learning

The Elements of Statistical Learning

Hastie, Tibshirani & Friedman · 2009

The canonical bridge between statistics and machine learning. Dense but enormously rewarding. The chapters on regularisation, trees, and ensemble methods remain the best in the literature.

An Introduction to Statistical Learning

James, Witten, Hastie & Tibshirani · 2013

The accessible companion to ESL — the right first book for students coming from economics who want to engage seriously with ML methods.

Pattern Recognition and Machine Learning

Christopher Bishop · 2006

The probabilistic perspective on machine learning — Bayesian inference, graphical models, and kernel methods treated with real depth. A reference I return to repeatedly.

Econometrics — Core texts

Econometric Analysis

William Greene · 8th ed.

The encyclopaedic reference. Not a book you read cover to cover, but one you keep within arm's reach. Every classical and semi-classical estimator is here, with derivations.

Econometric Theory and Methods

Davidson & MacKinnon · 2004

More modern and methodologically self-aware than Greene. The treatment of bootstrap methods and simulation-based inference is particularly strong.

A Guide to Modern Econometrics

Marno Verbeek · 5th ed.

My favourite introductory econometrics text. It strikes an unusually good balance between mathematical precision and applied intuition — the right starting point before moving to Greene or Davidson.

Econometrics — Causal inference

Mostly Harmless Econometrics

Angrist & Pischke · 2009

The book that made causal inference approachable for a generation of economists. The IV and RDD chapters in particular deserve to be read slowly.

Causal Inference: The Mixtape

Scott Cunningham · 2021

Freely available online. Cunningham writes with personality and the code examples are integrated seamlessly. The treatment of DiD and synthetic control is especially current.

Causality

Judea Pearl · 2000

The foundational text on structural causal models and do-calculus. Not an easy read, but it fundamentally reframes what identification means — a perspective every econometrician should encounter.

Econometrics — Text & Spatial

Speech and Language Processing

Jurafsky & Martin · 3rd ed. (online)

The standard NLP reference, freely available and continuously updated. The chapters on language models and word vectors are essential background for text-based econometrics.

Introduction to Spatial Econometrics

LeSage & Pace · 2009

The clearest treatment of spatial weight matrices, SAR models, and Bayesian spatial estimation. The construction and properties of W are handled more carefully here than anywhere else.

Spatial Econometrics: Methods and Models

Luc Anselin · 1988

The founding text of the field. Some notation has dated, but the conceptual architecture — how to think about spatial dependence as a modelling problem — remains indispensable.

Reading now

The Culture Map

Erin Meyer · 2014

A practical framework for understanding how cultural differences shape communication, trust, and decision-making in international professional contexts. Particularly resonant working across European academic environments.

Giving Back

I was lucky

Early in my life I understood something that not everyone gets to see in time: education is the only true social elevator. It does not care where you come from. It opens doors that nothing else can.

I was lucky enough to grasp this early, and to have people around me who confirmed it. But luck is not a system — and the Italian situation makes this painfully clear.

"The best investment you can make is in yourself." — Warren Buffett

Italy has one of the lowest rates of university graduation in Europe, and social mobility has been stalling for decades. Too many students — especially in the South — never encounter a convincing reason to believe that studying changes lives. Nobody around them has shown them that it can.

I try, in small ways, to be that example for someone else.

Share of 25–34 year-olds with a tertiary degree, EU countries, 2024

Source: Eurostat (edat_lfse_03; educ_uoe_grad04). STEM = science, mathematics, computing, engineering, manufacturing & construction.

The picture is stark. In 2024, only 31.6% of Italians aged 25–34 held a university degree — second-to-last in the EU, ahead only of Romania. The EU average stands at 44.2%. On STEM specifically, Italy produces around 19 graduates per 1,000 young people, while countries like Finland and Ireland produce more than 28. The gap is not narrowing fast enough.

Initiative

Christmas in STEM

Occasionally, I return to my former high school during the Christmas period to talk with students about how STEM subjects shape the world around us — from the algorithms that recommend their music to the models that price the risk behind their parents' mortgage. The goal is not recruitment. It is simply to make the invisible visible: to show that mathematics, statistics, and programming are not abstract exercises but genuine instruments of understanding and power.

I believe these conversations matter most when they happen early, before students have already closed doors in their own minds. If you are a teacher or school coordinator in Italy and would like to organise something similar, feel free to reach out.
